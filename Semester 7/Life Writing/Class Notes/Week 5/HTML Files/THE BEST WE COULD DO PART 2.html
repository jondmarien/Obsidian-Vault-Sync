Questioning Personal Truth

Chapter 4, "Blood and Rice," deals with Thi's relationship with her father.  It's a dynamic that, already, we've sensed lurking just beneath the surface of the narrative.  He was the primary care-provider for Thi, so it makes sense that a significant part of the text would be devoted to their relationship.


Pages 91-92: the panels depict Bui and her relationship with her father.  She explains it took a while to learn not to be afraid of her father, and on page 92, we see the Bui and her father sitting at tables together at different ages.   

Page 93: a splash page in which Bui's father is illustrated at the top as an adult living in Vietnam.  He is smoking, and the smoke trails down to an image of a pond in Vietnam.  

Thi states that "it took a long time to learn the right questions to ask" (92), which I find to be an intriguing point within the larger context of how we discuss trauma.  Here, Bui suggests that there are "right" and "wrong" ways to come to personal and family trauma narratives.  There is also the implication here that such information was not given freely--she had to earn the right to hear her father's history and engage with his story.  I'm reminded of a quotation by shame and vulnerability research, Bren√© Brown: "if we share our shame story with the wrong person, they can easily become one more piece of flying debris in an already dangerous storm."

The same can be said of trauma stories: if we share them (and, in so doing, relive them) with someone who is unsafe, then we can end up doing more harm than good to ourselves.  There is a risk inherent in opening up to people and sharing painful stories: they could refuse to believe you, respond poorly, feel personally attacked, be dismissive, etc.  There is also a risk that the audience could take on your narrative as part of their own through grief tourism or dark tourism/trauma tourism. While these terms have been used in the past to describe visiting physical locations of grief and trauma, recently they've been re-appropriated to include a type of metaphysical tourism through the narratives and experiences of others.  Below is a great video  (00:14:27) that discusses how grief tourism functions in a variety of traumatic contexts:

Narrative Ruptures

Chapter 5 fascinated me because of what it wasn't.  The captions on page 131 (the Chapter 5 title page) begin with: "Writing about my mother is harder for me--" and thus, would leave us to believe that this chapter would be about that relationship.  

Page 131: Bui sits at her drawing desk while staring at a photograph.  She appears frustrated.

But about half way through, Bui subverts the reader's expectations as the narrative is ruptured and shifts rather suddenly.  Bui's narrative of her mother's past seems to be progressing as one might expect until page 136, when we encounter a point where the narrative seems to fall apart and silence begins to take over:

Page 136: In panel one, Bui struggles to get her mother to speak to her about the past.  Panel two shows her mother speaking to Bui's husband in the passenger seat of the car, recounting stories about herself.

There is an underlying tension between Bui and her mother that translates into this narrative in the form of silences.  On page 136, there is no dialogue between the two women; rather, we read Thi's thoughts and reflections as she narrates.  Her mother is notably silent and even looking away from her daughter's gaze.  Thi is listening to recordings of her interviews with family members.  We don't know specifically if Thi is listening to M√° or another relative in this panel, but M√°'s body language is closed off and disengaged as though she fears her daughter's judgement. 

However, we learn that M√° is more willing to open up to Travis, Bui's husband.  However, it is significant that Bui depicts the conversation taking place in a car: M√° and Travis sit side-by-side and don't have to look at one another.  This approach is a psychological trick practiced by many parents: when having difficult conversations in the car, people often feel less intimidated and threatened because there isn't a social expectation to look each other in the eyes.

So, to return to our discussion about sharing trauma narratives, Thi gives the reader the impression here that M√° shares her past with Thi begrudgingly.  Thi hears her mother's narratives through some form of intermediary (audio recorder, her husband), and rarely do we see M√° have a conversation about the past with Thi directly.  M√°'s reluctance places Thi in the role of a voyeur or, we might even argue, of a grief tourist.  Thi highlights that her role was to listen and to and receive M√°'s narratives as a type of performance, but not participate actively in the narrative discourse.  

The more passive role that Thi occupies in this process is then juxtaposed starkly when M√°'s narrative is interrupted by Thi herself, who shifts our attention back to B·ªï's story:  

Page 151: the first panel shows Bui now thinking about her father as she shifts the narrative emphasis away from her mother's past and onto the one she knows better: her father's past.

Rather than devoting this entire chapter to her mother's past, Bui weaves in her father's history and how his presence would affect the trajectory of her mother's life.  Note the similarities and differences in how the relationship between B·ªï and Thi is depicted here, versus her relationship with her mother.  B·ªï tells his story while walking with Thi (again, side-by-side), but she is an active part of this narrative discourse.  She asks questions, and, on page 156, we even see B·ªï look at Thi obliquely as he answers her questions and even seems to anticipate criticism or judgments:

Page 153: Bui's father recounts his childhood becoming "westernized" after moving to Hai Ph√≤ng, the third largest city in Vietnam.

Page 158: the chapter shifts its focus to B·ªï's recollections and past when M√°'s silence takes over and her voice is lost in Bui's narrative

The part that I want to emphasize here is that the form in this chapter reflects its content: B·ªï's narrative takes over M√°'s, just as B·ªï took over for M√° as the primary caregiver for Thi.  The maternal voice is one of silence, where as the paternal voice is one of authority that speaks for the family's history.  Much like B·ªï dominated the household, his narrative and stories dominate Bui's as she attempts to understand and come to terms with her past.

Revisiting the Past

The concept of "THE PAST," or history, is often linked metaphorically with geography.  

Image Quote: "The past is a foreign country; the do things differently there."

Image Quote: "Yes, the past is another country, but one that we can visit, and once there we can bring back the things we need."

This idea of "visiting" and "touring" the past is one that emerges in B·ªï's narrative and aligns nicely with our previous discussion on grief tourism.  On pages 160-161, B·ªï recalls the painful experience of visiting his father and relatives:

Page 160: the panels show Bui's father visiting his relatives.  The relatives call him lazy and he reflects that they didn't like him very much.

Page 161: B·ªï recalls exploring northern Vietnam "as a tourist would."  He has an exchange with a small child who is cutting rubber tires to make sandals.  The child asks B·ªï why he doesn't work

But when we (re)visit the past, is it only the things that we need that come back with us?  I would argue that to revisit the past means that we must inevitably confront things that are detrimental to us as well.  Certainly, B·ªï seems to desire leaving the past (his father) behind him as he embarks upon a new phase of his life:

In fact, Chapter 6 demonstrates just how incongruent the past and the present often are, particularly in how people recollect and experience the two.  When Thi recalls visiting the first house in which she lived in Vietnam, we see just the past and the present come into conflict with each other:

Who Gets to Speak?

To pick up on the theme from the last section, we see how Thi attempts to compensate for what she perceives as almost a deficit: her lack of memory.  This problem is common enough in the humanities for those who study the past (history, literature, classics, etc.).  However, because historical records are fraught with bias and inaccuracies, it becomes difficult to navigate and reconcile the voices that get to speak in a narrative.  

Consider this: Bui is Vietnamese-American.  As such, Bui relies upon various sources as she researches the past, and many sources that she encounters are, at best, heavily biased; in actuality, many of these resources were Americentric or Eurocentric.  Thus, Bui's perspective and narrative cannot help but be coloured (pun intended) by the source materials that she uses, her own experiences, and her cultural paradigms.  We see her struggle with this restrictive view of history as she attempts to do research for this memoir:

Page 183: B·ªï brings Bui a video that covered the Vietnam war from an American perspective.  He indicates that there is footage of their old neighborhood on the video and that he can get her more, if she would like.
Page 184: The first panel depicts Bui watching the footage.  The second and third panels are illustrations meant to capture scenes from the video.  The final three panels show Bui reflecting and struggling with what she's seeing because she must depend upon the caricatures  of others since she doesn't have her own memories.
